
# Explanation: Higher-Order Thinking Question (HOTS)
## Mathematics – Symmetry (Class 6, CBSE)

---

### Question Recap
A figure has **rotational symmetry of order 4** but **no line of symmetry**.

Is such a figure possible? Explain your answer.

---

## Step-by-Step Explanation

### 1. What Does Rotational Symmetry of Order 4 Mean?
- A figure has **rotational symmetry of order 4** if it looks exactly the same **4 times** during a full turn of 360°.
- This means the figure matches itself after rotations of:
  - 90°
  - 180°
  - 270°
  - 360°

So, rotating the figure by **90°** about its centre brings it back to the same appearance.

---

### 2. What Is a Line of Symmetry?
- A **line of symmetry** is a line that divides a figure into **two identical mirror halves**.
- If a figure is folded along this line, both halves overlap exactly.
- If no such line exists, the figure has **no reflection (line) symmetry**.

---

### 3. Can Both Conditions Exist Together?
✅ **Yes, it is possible.**

- Rotational symmetry and line symmetry are **different properties**.
- A figure can match itself when rotated but still **fail to match** when folded along any line.
- Therefore, having rotational symmetry does **not guarantee** the presence of a line of symmetry.

---

### 4. Conceptual Example
- Imagine a figure with **four identical arms** arranged evenly around a central point.
- Each arm is bent or shaped in the **same direction**.
- When the figure is rotated by 90°, each arm takes the place of the next one → **rotational symmetry of order 4**.
- However, if you try to fold the figure along any straight line, the two halves do **not overlap exactly** → **no line of symmetry**.

---

## Final Conclusion
- ✅ The figure has **rotational symmetry of order 4** because it matches itself after every 90° rotation.
- ❌ The figure has **no line of symmetry** because it cannot be divided into two identical mirror halves.
- ✅ Therefore, **such a figure is possible**.

---

### Teaching Tip (Optional)
- Ask students to draw a pinwheel-like figure.
- Rotate the paper by 90° and observe.
- Try folding along different lines to check for mirror symmetry.

This helps students clearly distinguish between **rotation** and **reflection** symmetry.
